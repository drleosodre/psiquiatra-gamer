Grief in games: why it hurts when a character dies

Leonardo Sodré · psychiatrist, psychoanalyst and gamer ~6 min read

Plenty of people who "never cry at anything" have found themselves choking up at the death of a video game character. And then felt a little sheepish about it: it was just a game. But the pain was real, the lump in the throat was real. There's nothing ridiculous about it, and psychoanalysis explains why. What you lived through there has a precise name: mourning. And mourning, as Freud described it in 1917, is a labor.

Mourning is work, not weakness

In Mourning and Melancholia, Freud made a simple and profound observation: when we lose someone (or something) we loved, it isn't enough to "accept it" and move on. The mind has a task to carry out, one he called the work of mourning. We had invested affection in that person, Freud speaks of investing "libido" in the loved object. When the object is lost, that affection is left with nowhere to go, and the mind must, little by little, withdraw it, memory by memory. It's painful and exhausting precisely because it is labor: revisiting the bond so as to be able, at last, to let it go.

Why we get attached to pixels

"But it was only a character." Yes, and you spent hours, sometimes dozens of hours, at their side. You cared for them, depended on them, rooted for them. In that time, you invested real affection in a figure that, though fictional, occupied a genuine affective place. The mind doesn't keep as precise an account between "real" and "fictional" as we'd like: wherever there was a bond, loss exacts the work of mourning. That's why the death of a companion on a long RPG journey can hurt more than that of a distant person in real life. It isn't a lack of perspective. It's the measure of the bond.

When the game makes you lose for real

The most powerful games know this exactly and turn it to the story's advantage. A death you can't prevent, a sacrifice the script forces you to commit, a companion who doesn't come back, these are moments when the game turns the player from spectator into mourner. And there's a detail psychoanalysis prizes: in a game, you often don't just watch the loss, you enact it (you press the button, you make the choice). This mixes mourning with guilt, and that's where the experience becomes unforgettable, the game makes you work through not just the absence, but your own part in it.

The grief that crosses the screen

There's also a quieter and more valuable function. Living through a controlled loss, inside a game, can be a safe rehearsal for the losses of life, a place where we practice feeling, naming, and going on. It doesn't replace real mourning, of course, but it offers a protected space to touch this difficult emotion without falling apart. Freud distinguished mourning (which passes) from melancholia (which sticks and sickens); a game, when well made, stays on the side of mourning: it hurts, it works through, and it hands us back to life a little more able to feel.

Crying over a character is taking the game seriously

So the next time someone laughs at you for getting emotional over a character's death, you have your answer: that wasn't an overreaction, it was the work of mourning doing its job. A game capable of making you feel a real loss didn't leave you fragile, it reminded you that you're someone who bonds, who invests affection, who feels. And that, on screen or off, is the most alive thing there is.
